Q29B  Contribution Margin % (Standalone)

Sales=$100K. Variable costs=$70K. Fixed costs=$60K.
a) CM in dollars? b) CM%? c) Net income/loss?

a) CM = Sales − VC = $100K − $70K = $30,000
b) CM% = (Sales − VC) / Sales = $30K / $100K = 30%
c) Net Income = CM − FC = $30K − $60K = −$30K (loss)

FORMULA CARD — keep these 5 straight:
CM (dollars)  = Sales − Variable Costs
CM%            = (Sales − VC) / Sales         ← USE THIS FOR MCQs
DOL            = CM / EBIT
EBIT           = CM − Fixed Costs
Net Income     = EBIT − Interest − Taxes

Q53  Break-Even Analysis (Quantity & Dollar)

TFC=$120K. Price=$50/unit. VC=$30/unit.
a) BE quantity? b) BE dollars? c) BE if price drops to $40?

a) BE Qty = TFC / (P − VC) = $120K / ($50−$30) = 6,000 units
b) BE $ = TFC / CM ratio = $120K / (($50−$30)/$50) = $120K / 40% = $300K
   OR: BE $ = BE Qty × P = 6,000 × $50 = $300K ✓
c) New BE Qty = $120K / ($40−$30) = 12,000 units (doubles!)

Q54  Founder Trilemma

A startup has 12-month runway, 20% monthly growth, needs $2M to scale.
What are the three trade-offs? Which two can it optimize at once?

TRILEMMA: Runway × Growth × Capital — can only optimize TWO at once.

Options:
(1) Maximize Runway + Growth: Cut burn → slow growth, survive longer
(2) Maximize Growth + Capital: Raise now → dilute, but grow fast
(3) Maximize Runway + Capital: Raise small → runway extended, growth capped

Implication: Startups must choose their trade-off explicitly.
Most VCs expect you to optimize Growth + Capital (raise enough to hit next milestone).

Q55  18-Month Runway Rationale

Why do VCs expect 18-month minimum runway?

18 mo = 6–9 mo (fundraise cycle) + 6–9 mo (buffer to next milestone)

Why 6–9 months for fundraising?
• VC due diligence takes 2–3 months
• Term sheet negotiation: 1–2 months
• Legal docs + closing: 1–2 months
• First investor meeting to signed term sheet: 3–6 months typical

Buffer needed because:
• If raise takes 9 months, you need 9 months cash to survive it
• Most startups underestimate time-to-close by 2–3×

Rule: Runway = 12 months → fundraising starts NOW (dangerous)
Rule: Runway > 18 months → healthy enough to negotiate from strength

Q56  CLV with CAC Payback Period

ARPU=$100/mo. Gross margin=70%. Churn=5%/mo.
CAC=$300 (one-time acquisition cost).
a) CLV? b) Lifespan? c) Payback period?

a) CLV = (ARPU × GM%) × (1/Churn) = ($100 × 70%) × (1/0.05)
   CLV = $70 × 20 months = $1,400
b) Lifespan = 1/Churn = 1/0.05 = 20 months
c) Payback = CAC / (ARPU × GM%) = $300 / $70 = 4.29 months

CLV:CAC = $1,400/$300 = 4.67x  (above 3:1 threshold → unit economics healthy)
Payback < 12 months is generally acceptable; < 6 months is strong.

Q57  Quick VC Method

Startup needs $500K. VC wants 25% post-money.
a) Post-money? b) Pre-money? c) Founder ownership %?

a) Post-money = Investment / Ownership% = $500K / 25% = $2,000,000
b) Pre-money = Post-money − Investment = $2M − $500K = $1,500,000
c) Founder% = Pre-money / Post-money = $1.5M / $2M = 75%

Quick VC formula (same as Q3):
Post-money = Investment ÷ Target_Ownership%
Pre-money = Post-money − Investment
Founder% = Pre-money ÷ Post-money

Q58  TAM / SAM / SOM — 4 Estimation Methods

Market = 50M users. ARPU=$60/yr. 35% online. Target: 8% of online in Yr3.
SOM capture rate=20% of achievable.

TAM / SAM / SOM (standard):
TAM = 50M × $60 = $3B/yr
SAM = 50M × 35% × $60 = $1.05B/yr
SOM = 50M × 35% × 8% × $60 = $84M/yr

FOUR ESTIMATION METHODS:
(1) Top-down: Start with TAM → apply demographic/geographic filters → SAM → apply penetration assumptions → SOM
    e.g., TAM $3B → filter to online users → SAM $1.05B → assume 8% penetration → SOM $84M

(2) Bottom-up: Start with # customers × price from specific segments
    e.g., 1M users × $80 ARPU = $80M + 500K users × $40 = $100M total

(3) External-in: Use industry reports (Gartner, Forrester, Statista) for TAM
    → adjust down for your specific segment → SAM
    → adjust for competition/market share → SOM

(4) Value Theory: Estimate value created for customers × # potential customers
    e.g., Saves $500/yr per user × 200K users = $100M addressable value
    → SOM = fraction of total value you can capture

SOM key insight: SOM ≠ % of SAM. SOM = f(competition, sales capacity, product maturity, CAC).
Your SOM in Year 1 << SAM × assumed %. Grows as product-market fit proven.

E14. A VC owns 2M shares at $5.00/share (invested $10M).
A down round is being priced at $3.00/share.
The startup issues 1M new shares to new investors.
a) What is a weighted average anti-dilution clause? (3 marks)
b) Calculate the new price per share and VC's new share count. (4 marks)
c) Compare to full ratchet protection — which is better for the VC? (3 marks)

a) WEIGHTED AVERAGE ANTI-DILUTION:
• Also called "broad-based" anti-dilution
• Protects VC if new shares are issued at a LOWER price than VC paid
• Formula: New Price = Old Price × [(Old Shares + New Equivalent) / (Old Shares + New Shares)]
• "New Equivalent" = $ new investment / old price (how many shares VC would have at old price)
• More protective than nothing, less protective than full ratchet

b) WEIGHTED AVERAGE CALCULATION:
Old shares = 2,000,000   Old price = $5.00
New shares issued = 1,000,000   New price = $3.00
New investment = 1M × $3 = $3,000,000

Step 1: New Equivalent shares = $3M / $5.00 = 600,000 shares
         (at old price, same $3M would buy 600K shares)

Step 2: New Price = $5.00 × [(2M + 600K) / (2M + 1M)]
                     = $5.00 × (2.6M / 3.0M)
                     = $5.00 × 0.867
                     = $4.33/share

Step 3: VC new shares = $10M / $4.33 = 2,309,000 shares (rounded)
         OR: 2M × ($5/$4.33) = 2,309,000 shares ✓

VC ownership before: 2M/(2M+1M) = 66.7%
VC ownership after (no protection): 2M/3M = 50%
VC ownership after (weighted avg): 2.309M/3.309M = 69.8%

c) FULL RATCHET vs WEIGHTED AVERAGE:
Full ratchet: VC resets ALL old shares to new lower price ($3.00)
  → VC new shares = $10M / $3.00 = 3,333,333 shares
  → VC ownership = 3.333M/4.333M = 76.9%

Weighted average (Q14 above): VC gets 2,309,000 shares = 69.8%

Full ratchet is MORE protective for VC (more extra shares, less dilution).
Weighted average is a COMPROMISE — gives VC some protection without devastating founder dilution.
In practice, broad-based weighted average is the most common anti-dilution clause.

E15. A VC invested $8M at $10/share = 800,000 shares.
A down round prices new shares at $4/share. The startup raises $2M new capital.
a) Calculate VC's new share count under full ratchet. (4 marks)
b) Show the VC's effective price per share before and after. (3 marks)
c) Why do founders strongly prefer weighted average over full ratchet? (3 marks)

a) FULL RATCHET CALCULATION:
Full ratchet: ALL old shares reset to the new (lower) price.

New price = $4.00/share
VC new share count = $8M / $4.00 = 2,000,000 shares
VC receives: 2,000,000 − 800,000 = 1,200,000 EXTRA shares (for free!)

b) EFFECTIVE PRICE COMPARISON:
Before down round:
  VC paid: $8M / 800,000 = $10.00/share
  VC stake in startup: 800,000 shares

After full ratchet:
  VC holds: 2,000,000 shares
  VC effective cost: $8M / 2,000,000 = $4.00/share
  VC is now FULLY PROTECTED at the new lower valuation

Without anti-dilution:
  VC would have 800,000 shares worth $4.00 = $3.2M value → LOSS of $4.8M
With full ratchet:
  VC has 2,000,000 shares worth $4.00 = $8M value → FULLY PROTECTED

c) WHY FOUNDERS PREFER WEIGHTED AVERAGE:
Full ratchet is devastating for founders because:
• Any down round (even a small one) triggers massive dilution to VCs
• Founders may avoid necessary fundraising to escape full ratchet → company dies
• Weighted average: dilution is proportional and limited
• Full ratchet = extreme outlier protection for VCs, not for founders
• In practice: weighted average is market standard; full ratchet is rare and founders should negotiate hard against it

E16. A startup is estimating its TAM/SAM/SOM for a food delivery app in SEA.
Total addressable market for food delivery = $20B/yr.
Only 40% of SEA population has smartphone access.
Startups estimates 15% of reachable users in Year 3.
a) Explain the four TAM/SAM/SOM estimation methods. (4 marks)
b) Calculate TAM, SAM, SOM using the top-down approach. (3 marks)
c) What factors make SOM different from 15% of SAM? (3 marks)

a) FOUR ESTIMATION METHODS:
(1) TOP-DOWN: Start with global/industry TAM → apply filters (geography, segment, 
    technology) → reach SAM → apply penetration assumptions → SOM
    e.g., Global smartphone market → filter to SEA → filter to urban → SOM

(2) BOTTOM-UP: Start from specific customers × price. Build up from unit economics.
    Count potential customers in target segment × expected spend = SAM
    Then apply market share assumption for SOM
    e.g., 10M potential users in SG/MY/TH × $50/mo × 12 = $6B SAM
    SOM = 10M × 3% penetration × $50/mo × 12 = $180M

(3) EXTERNAL-IN (market research): Use published reports (Gartner, Forrester, 
    Euromonitor, Statista) for TAM and SAM figures
    Then adjust down for your specific segment and competition
    e.g., TAM from Gartner = $20B food delivery SEA → SAM = 40% smartphone = $8B
    SOM = $8B × assumed market share

(4) VALUE THEORY: How much value do you create for customers × number of customers
    e.g., App saves user $30/week on food × 52 weeks = $1,560/yr value
    × 5M users willing to pay = $7.8B value created → TAM based on value delivered

b) TOP-DOWN CALCULATIONS:
TAM = $20B/yr (full food delivery market)
SAM = $20B × 40% (smartphone penetration) = $8B/yr
SOM = $8B × 15% (Yr3 capture of reachable market) = $1.2B/yr

c) WHY SOM ≠ 15% OF SAM:
SOM is constrained by REALITY, not just market size:
• Current user base: if you have 100K users today, SOM Yr1 << 15% of SAM
• Sales capacity: how many users can your sales team acquire per month?
• CAC: if your CAC is too high relative to CLV, you can't scale fast
• Competition: GrabFood, Foodpanda already dominate; new entrant gets scraps
• Product maturity: early product captures only early adopters (2-5% of segment)
• Geography: may only launch in 1 city first, not full SAM region

Rule: SOM ≤ min(current_capacity, achievable_market_share × SAM)
SOM grows over time as unit economics prove and sales capacity scales.

E17. A startup's DCF valuation is sensitive to:
WACC = 12%, FCF = $500K, g = 3%
a) Calculate PV of perpetuity at base case. (2 marks)
b) If WACC rises to 14%, what is % change in value? (3 marks)
c) If FCF is overstated by 20%, what is % change in value? (3 marks)
d) What does this tell you about DCF sensitivity for early-stage ventures? (2 marks)

a) BASE CASE PV:
PV = FCF / (WACC − g) = $500K / (12% − 3%) = $500K / 9% = $5.56M

b) WACC SENSITIVITY (+2% from 12% to 14%):
PV = $500K / (14% − 3%) = $500K / 11% = $4.55M
Change: ($4.55M − $5.56M) / $5.56M = −18.2% value drop
A 2% rise in WACC → 18% fall in DCF value. HIGH SENSITIVITY.

c) FCF OVERSTATED BY 20%:
Adjusted FCF = $500K × 80% = $400K
PV = $400K / 9% = $4.44M
Change: ($4.44M − $5.56M) / $5.56M = −20.1% value drop
20% overstatement in FCF → 20% overstatement in value (linear)

d) WHY THIS MATTERS FOR EARLY-STAGE:
• Small changes in WACC or growth rate cause LARGE changes in value
• Most value is in terminal value (vs near-term FCFs)
• Early-stage FCFs are uncertain → DCF is unreliable as primary valuation method
• Better to use multiple methods: DCF (sensitized), comps, VC method, step-up
• For exam: always show sensitivity table when doing DCF for startups

SENSITIVITY TABLE (WACC × g):
                  g = 2%     g = 3%     g = 4%
WACC = 12%       $5.00M    $5.56M    $6.25M
WACC = 13%       $4.55M    $5.00M    $5.56M
WACC = 14%       $4.17M    $4.55M    $5.00M

FC-1  LIQUIDITY RATIOS

Current Ratio  = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
Quick Ratio    = (Current Assets − Inventory) / CL
Cash Ratio     = (Cash + Marketable Securities) / CL

Example: CA=$500K, Inventory=$100K, CL=$300K
  Current Ratio = $500K/$300K = 1.67x
  Quick Ratio   = ($500K−$100K)/$300K = 1.33x
  Cash Ratio    = $200K/$300K = 0.67x

Rule: Current Ratio > 1.5 is generally acceptable for startups.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FC-2  PROFITABILITY MARGINS

Gross Margin     = Gross Profit / Net Sales        = (Sales − COGS) / Sales
EBIT Margin      = EBIT / Net Sales               = (Sales − COGS − Fixed Costs) / Sales
EBITDA Margin    = EBITDA / Net Sales              = EBIT + D&A / Sales
Net Margin       = Net Income / Net Sales

Example: Sales=$1M, COGS=$400K, D&A=$50K, Interest=$30K, Tax=25%
  Gross Profit  = $1M − $400K = $600K
  Gross Margin = $600K/$1M = 60%
  EBIT         = $600K − FC (say $200K) = $400K
  EBIT Margin  = $400K/$1M = 40%
  EBITDA       = $400K + $50K = $450K
  EBITDA Margin= $450K/$1M = 45%
  EBT          = $400K − $30K = $370K
  Tax          = $370K × 25% = $92.5K
  Net Income   = $370K − $92.5K = $277.5K
  Net Margin   = $277.5K/$1M = 27.75%

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FC-3  FREE CASH FLOW (FCF)

FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capex

Operating CF = EBIT × (1 − tax) + D&A − ΔWorking Capital

Simplified (no D&A or WC change):
FCF ≈ EBIT × (1 − tax) − Capex − ΔNet Working Capital

Example: EBIT=$500K, tax=25%, D&A=$50K, ΔWC=+$20K, Capex=$80K
  Operating CF = $500K × 0.75 + $50K − $20K = $405K
  FCF          = $405K − $80K = $325K

Key point: FCF is "free for distribution" — what's left after reinvesting in the business.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FC-4  NOPAT & CAPITAL CHARGE

NOPAT         = EBIT × (1 − tax rate)
Capital Charge = WACC × Capital Employed
EVA            = NOPAT − Capital Charge

Note: Q20 uses Capital Employed = Total Assets. More precisely:
  Capital Employed = Debt + Equity = Total Assets

Example: EBIT=$1M, tax=30%, WACC=10%, Capital=$8M
  NOPAT         = $1M × 0.70 = $700K
  Capital Charge= 10% × $8M = $800K
  EVA           = $700K − $800K = −$100K (destroys value)

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FC-5  DDM — REARRANGED (Find Ke or P0)

Standard:    P0 = D1 / (Ke − g)
Rearranged: Ke = D1 / P0 + g

Example: D1=$3.15, P0=$63.75, g=5%
  Ke = $3.15/$63.75 + 5% = 4.94% + 5% = 9.94% ≈ 10%

Use when: You know the market price and dividend — solve for required return.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FC-6  EXIT MULTIPLE ↔ IRR TABLE

Use this table to convert between IRR and exit multiples:

  Exit Multiple = (1 + IRR)^Years

  IRR    2 yr    3 yr    4 yr    5 yr
  ─────────────────────────────
   30%   1.69x   2.20x   2.86x   3.71x
   40%   1.96x   2.74x   3.84x   5.38x
   50%   2.25x   3.38x   5.06x   7.59x
   60%   2.56x   4.10x   6.55x  10.49x

Example: VC needs 50% IRR over 5 years → target 7.5x exit multiple
  Check: (1.50)^5 = 7.59x ✓

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FC-7  CLV:CAC INTERPRETATION GUIDE

CLV:CAC ratio tells you if unit economics are sustainable:

  Ratio        Interpretation                Action
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
  > 4:1        Blue zone — very strong     Consider accelerating spend
  3:1 – 4:1   Green zone — healthy        Good to scale
  2:1 – 3:1   Yellow zone — acceptable    Improve before scaling
  < 2:1        Red zone — unsustainable    Fix unit economics first

Example: CLV=$1,400, CAC=$300 → CLV:CAC = 4.67x (blue zone ✓)

Also watch: CAC Payback Period
  Payback (months) = CAC / (ARPU × Gross Margin%)
  Target: < 12 months (< 6 months is strong)

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FC-8  WACC — EXPLICIT FORMULA

WACC = (Equity/(D+E)) × Ke  +  (Debt/(D+E)) × Kd × (1−t)

Where:
  Ke = Rf + β × ERP       (CAPM — cost of equity)
  Kd = pre-tax cost of debt
  t  = corporate tax rate
  (D/E) = market value debt-to-equity ratio

Example: E/V=60%, Ke=12%, D/V=40%, Kd=8%, tax=25%
  After-tax Kd = 8% × (1−0.25) = 6%
  WACC = 60%×12% + 40%×6% = 7.2% + 2.4% = 9.6%

Common mistake: Using book value weights vs. market value weights.
Always use MARKET value of equity = share price × shares outstanding.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FC-9  ADDITIONAL FINANCIAL RATIOS

ROA (Return on Assets)     = Net Income / Total Assets
                            → How efficiently assets generate profit

Interest Coverage Ratio     = EBIT / Interest Expense
                            → Can the firm cover its interest payments?
                            → < 1.5x is a warning sign; < 1x means loss

Debt to Assets             = Total Debt / Total Assets
                            → What % of assets are financed by debt
                            → > 50% means more than half the firm is debt-financed

Financial Leverage (EM)    = Total Assets / Shareholders' Equity
                            → Same as Equity Multiplier in DuPont ROE

Asset Turnover             = Net Sales / Total Assets
                            → How efficiently assets generate revenue

Fixed Asset Turnover       = Net Sales / Fixed Assets
                            → For asset-heavy businesses (manufacturing, telcos)

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FC-10  VC PV APPROACH — EXPLICIT FORMULA

VC FV approach (Q2):
  Acquired% = Investment × (1+r)^T / (P/E × Earnings_at_exit)

VC PV approach (equivalent — discount future ownership):
  Acquired% = Investment / [P/E × Earnings_T / (1+r)^T]

These two are algebraically equivalent — use whichever is easier.

Example (same as Q2):
  Investment=$1M, r=50%, T=5, P/E=10x, Earnings_T=$3.8M
  FV: Acquired% = $1M×(1.50)^5 / (10×$3.8M) = $7.59M/$38M = 19.97% ✓
  PV: Acquired% = $1M / [$10×$3.8M/(1.50)^5] = $1M/$4.81M = 20.8% ≈ 20% ✓

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FC-11  TAM/SAM/SOM — SLIDE FORMULA VARIANT

TAM = Total customers × Average annual sales per customer
SAM = Reachable customers × Average customer value
SOM = Last year's market share × SAM     (slide version)

Slide version uses: SOM = last_yr_market_share × SAM
This differs from the % capture approach in the cheatsheet.
Both are valid — the slide version assumes you already have some market share.

Revised SOM formula (most accurate):
SOM = min(current_capacity, achievable_penetration × SAM)

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FC-12  PV APPROACH — SHARES FORMULA VARIANT

VC method — shares to issue:
  n = Original_Shares × Acquired% / (1 − Acquired%)
     (same as Q6/Q46)

VC method — ownership check:
  VC% = n / (Original + n)
  Founder% = Original / (Original + n)
